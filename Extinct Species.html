EXTINCT SPECIES IN 2024

Rhinocer


RHINOCER

Rhinoceros are a critically endangered species with less than 30,000 rhino living in the wild today. At the start of the 20th century, there were over 500,000. Human activity has caused this dramatic decline in rhino numbers. Initially, numbers dropped due to hunting, but today the main threats to rhino are poaching and habitat loss.

VAQUITA


VAQUITA

Vaquita, the world's rarest marine mammal, is on the edge of extinction. The plight of cetaceans—whales—as a whole is exemplified by the rapid decline of the vaquita.We are on the brink of losing them forever. Vaquita are often caught and drowned in gillnets used by illegal fishing operations in marine protected areas.

BENGAL TIGER

BENGAL TIGER


This big cat once roamed Asia, from the eastern coast of Russia to the Caspian Sea. However, since the beginning of the 20th century, an estimated 97 per cent of the world's wild tigers have been lost. There are currently around 4,500 remaining in the wild across Asia, making the tiger an endangered species.

GAUR

GAUR

It is regionally extinct in Sri Lanka and possibly in Bangladesh. The gaur has been thriving in protected areas. The Gaur population in India was estimated to be at 12000-22000 in the mid-1990s. They are primarily found in hilly regions of South India like Wayanad, Nagarhole, Mudumalai and Bandipur.

SUMATRAN ORANGUTAN

SUMATRAN ORANGUTAN

Derived from the Malay words for “person of the forest," Sumatran orangutans are critically endangered. The Sumatran orangutan is almost exclusively arboreal, living among the trees of tropical rainforests. Females virtually never travel on the ground and adult males do so rarely.

NILGIRI TAHR

NILGIRI TAHR

The Nilgiri tahr, which used to be found along the entire stretch of Western Ghats, is presently found only in small fragmented pockets. A large part of its population has been wiped out from its historical range. The existing populations are under severe stress due to habitat loss and hunting.
